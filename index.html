Essay

And suddenly I was standing on a podium in front of hundreds inside the United Nations General Assembly Building, receiving my first standing ovation. After all the sacrifice, every choice felt worth it , the second place I had earned within the United Nations Games 2025, and later, first place at an IBJJF Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu Kids Open. Through chess, I learned that the hardest opponent was never the person across the board. It was myself.

Both of those medals belonged to my years in the United States, the country that shaped me academically and personally.

Then I woke up, not from a dream, but from that stage of my life. My time there had ended, and leaving was one of the hardest things I have ever done. Returning to Kazakhstan was difficult, not only because I missed my friends, but because I felt like I had to start over. I remember sitting in the U.S. history class, hesitant to raise my hand, worried that my accent would make me stand out. A few weeks later, I spoke up, joining discussions, and realizing that my experiences were not something to hide, they gave me things I could share with others.

That same drive had carried me through my years in New York. At PS/IS 78Q and Manhattan Village Academy. I received the Principal's Award for Academic Excellence while having an average of 95%. All those nights studying were worth it yet again. That stretch also included the Artificial Intelligence program at the City College of New York STEM Institute, where I won the Best Project and Leadership Awards, alongside competing in national and international chess and Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu tournaments and playing varsity soccer.

None of it taught me that success is only about medals or certificates. The real success is the discipline to keep sitting across the board, or the mat, after losing. My interest in everything grew less from the awards themselves and more from the setbacks that came before them.

I am looking for a school that will ask the same of me. CIS Astana's dual Canadian-Kazakh curriculum feels like the right place to keep doing that , it does not ask me to choose between the international education that challenged me abroad and the country I am rebuilding my place in. I really like what CIS offers students. For example, double diplomas, which are helpful for those aiming at a Canadian college or university. I want to keep growing in a place like that, while staying close to my family, and to contribute through my leadership, and willingness to learn, proudly representing CIS in chess, Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu, technology, and mathematics competitions.

The standing ovation in the United Nations building lasted only a few moments. But they changed the way I think and gave me more confidence for the future struggle with my toughest opponent, which is myself. I want to become a better version of myself and share this experience with my future classmates.
