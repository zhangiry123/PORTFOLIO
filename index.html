Essay

And suddenly I was standing on the podium in front of thousands of people inside the United Nations building. It was the first time I had ever received a standing ovation. After years of discipline and sacrifice, every choice felt worth it. Those efforts helped me earn second place at the United Nations Games Chess Tournament and later first place at an IBJJF Brazilian Jiu Jitsu Open. Through chess, I learned that the hardest opponent was never the person across the board. It was myself. Then I woke up. Not from a dream, but from one stage of my life. My years in the United States had ended. Leaving the country that shaped me academically and personally was one of the hardest things I have ever done. Returning to Kazakhstan was difficult, not only because I missed my friends and teachers, but also because I felt like I had to start over. I remember sitting in class, hesitant to raise my hand, worried that my accent or different experiences would make me stand out. That moment of insecurity taught me humility and reminded me that growth often begins with discomfort. At PS/IS 78Q and Manhattan Village Academy, I was recognized among the top twenty freshman students and received the Principal's Award for Academic Excellence. I also earned a Certificate of Academic Excellence in Spanish. Beyond academics, I competed in national and international chess and Brazilian Jiu Jitsu tournaments, played varsity soccer, and completed the Artificial Intelligence program at the City College of New York STEM Institute, where I received both the Best Project Award and the Leadership Award. These experiences taught me to stay calm under pressure, work well with others, and keep improving. More importantly, they showed me that success is not only about medals or certificates, but also about resilience and the lessons learned from failure and doubt. I discovered my interest in computer science, economics, and applied mathematics not only through awards, but through solving problems, facing setbacks, and learning from them. Each challenge reminded me that growth comes from persistence and that leadership means encouraging others even when success is not guaranteed. I chose CIS because its Canadian curriculum emphasizes critical thinking and collaboration. After studying in the United States, I value an environment that encourages curiosity and independence, and I believe CIS offers that opportunity while allowing me to stay close to my family. I hope to contribute to the school through my work ethic, leadership, and willingness to learn. I also look forward to sharing my experiences with my classmates and proudly representing CIS in chess competitions, Brazilian Jiu Jitsu tournaments, and technology and mathematics competitions. I know I will not succeed every time, but I will never give up. That is the mindset I will bring to the CIS community.
